Why are my plant's leaves turning yellow? 7 causes ranked

By Dr. GreenFix · 6 min read

Yellow leaves are the single most common photo we receive in a Photo Diagnosis booking — and the frustrating truth is that yellowing can mean almost anything. The good news: the pattern of yellowing usually narrows it down fast.

1. Overwatering (most common)

Look for yellowing that starts on lower, older leaves, feels soft or mushy, and is paired with soil that stays wet for days. Roots sitting in water can't breathe, and the plant starts shutting down leaves to cope.

2. Underwatering

Yellowing here comes with dry, crispy edges and a light, dry pot. The whole plant often looks limp before leaves turn.

3. Nitrogen deficiency

Uniform pale yellowing across the whole leaf, starting with the oldest growth, is a classic sign the plant has run out of nitrogen and is pulling it from older leaves to feed new ones.

4. Iron or magnesium deficiency

If the leaf veins stay green while the tissue between them turns yellow, that's "interveinal chlorosis" — usually iron or magnesium, often in plants grown in the wrong soil pH.

  • 5. Too much direct sun — scorched, yellow-to-brown patches on the sun-facing side only.
  • 6. Natural ageing — a single old leaf near the base turning yellow and dropping is often just normal leaf turnover.
  • 7. Pests — check the underside of yellowing leaves for tiny dots, webbing, or a sticky residue (honeydew) before ruling this out.

If you've worked through this list and you're still not sure, that's exactly what a Photo Diagnosis is for — send 4–8 clear photos and we'll give you a definite cause and treatment plan within 24 hours.

The 6 garden pests we see most in Melbourne (and how to beat them)

By Dr. GreenFix · 8 min read

Most Melbourne garden pest problems fall into one of six categories. Here's how to spot each one and what actually works — starting with the least toxic option first.

Aphids

Small, pear-shaped insects clustering on new growth and flower buds, often green or black. Leaves may curl or become sticky with honeydew. Fix: a strong jet of water every few days, or an insecticidal soap spray for bigger infestations.

Snails & slugs

Irregular holes in leaves and a silvery slime trail, worst after rain. Fix: beer traps, crushed eggshell barriers, or hand-picking at dusk when they're most active.

Caterpillars

Chewed, ragged holes and visible droppings (small dark pellets) on leaves. Fix: hand removal is often enough for small gardens; for brassicas, netting prevents the moths from laying eggs in the first place.

Scale insects

Small, immobile brown or white bumps along stems that don't brush off. Fix: horticultural oil smothers them effectively; scrape off heavy infestations first with a soft brush.

Spider mites

Fine webbing and tiny speckled dots on leaves, worse in hot, dry conditions. Fix: increase humidity, hose down foliage regularly, and use miticide only if it's severe.

Whitefly

A cloud of tiny white insects flies up when you disturb the plant. Fix: yellow sticky traps plus insecticidal soap on the underside of leaves.

Fertiliser

N-P-K explained: a fertiliser guide that actually makes sense

By Dr. GreenFix · 7 min read

Every fertiliser bag has three numbers on it, like 10-5-8. That's the N-P-K ratio, and once you understand what each number does, choosing the right feed becomes simple.

N — Nitrogen

Drives leafy, green growth. High-nitrogen feeds suit lawns and leafy vegetables like lettuce and spinach. Too much nitrogen on flowering plants gives you lots of leaves and few flowers.

P — Phosphorus

Supports root development and flowering. Higher phosphorus is ideal at planting time and for flowering or fruiting plants like tomatoes and roses.

K — Potassium

Builds overall plant strength, disease resistance, and fruit quality. Important year-round, especially as plants move into fruiting or stressful weather.

A simple feeding schedule

  • Leafy vegetables & lawns: higher N (e.g. 12-4-8), every 4–6 weeks in growing season.
  • Flowering & fruiting plants (roses, tomatoes): balanced or higher P-K (e.g. 5-10-10), every 3–4 weeks.
  • Indoor houseplants: a balanced, diluted liquid feed (e.g. 10-10-10 at half strength) monthly during spring/summer, none in winter.
  • Native Australian plants: use a low-phosphorus native fertiliser — many natives are phosphorus-sensitive and can be damaged by standard blends.

Yellowing between the veins on new growth despite regular feeding usually points to a micronutrient gap (iron, manganese) rather than N-P-K — a chelated trace element feed solves this in most cases.

Seasonal

Melbourne seasonal planting guide: what to plant every month

By Dr. GreenFix · 9 min read

Melbourne's climate can genuinely deliver four seasons in one day, which trips up even experienced gardeners moving from other cities. Here's a simple month-grouped guide.

Spring (Sep–Nov)

Prime planting season. Get tomatoes, herbs, and summer annuals in after the last frost risk passes (typically mid-October in Melbourne). Divide and repot perennials now.

Summer (Dec–Feb)

Focus on watering consistency over new planting — Melbourne summers swing between mild and extreme heat days. Mulch heavily to protect roots, and plant heat-tolerant natives in the cooler evenings.

Autumn (Mar–May)

One of the best planting windows of the year — the soil is still warm but the harsh heat has passed. Ideal for trees, shrubs, and cool-season vegetables like broccoli and peas.

Winter (Jun–Aug)

Bare-root fruit trees and roses go in now while dormant. Reduce watering on most plants, and hold off feeding until active growth resumes in spring.

Indoor Plants

Fiddle leaf fig dropping leaves? Here's the real fix

By Dr. GreenFix · 5 min read

Fiddle leaf figs are our most-requested indoor plant diagnosis, and it's almost never one single cause. Work through these in order.

1. Check the watering routine first

Fiddles hate both extremes. Water only when the top 5cm of soil is dry, then water deeply until it drains out the bottom. Never let the pot sit in a saucer of water.

2. Then check light

They need bright, indirect light — a spot within a couple of metres of a north or east-facing window works well. Too little light causes slow, leggy growth and leaf drop.

3. Rule out drafts and temperature swings

Fiddles dislike sitting near air-conditioning vents, heaters, or draughty doors — sudden temperature changes are a common trigger for sudden leaf drop.

4. Check the roots last

If the first three check out, gently ease the plant from its pot and look for mushy, dark roots (overwatering/rot) versus tightly circling roots (rootbound, needs repotting).

How to test your soil drainage in 10 minutes (no kit needed)

By Dr. GreenFix · 4 min read

Poor drainage is one of the most common — and most overlooked — reasons new plantings fail. This simple test tells you what you're working with before you plant anything.

The percolation test

  • Dig a hole roughly 30cm deep and 30cm wide in the area you plan to plant.
  • Fill it completely with water and let it drain fully once (this saturates the surrounding soil).
  • Fill it again, and time how long it takes to drain.

Reading the result

Draining within 1–4 hours is ideal for most garden plants. Faster than an hour means very free-draining, sandy soil — great for natives and Mediterranean plants, but you'll need to water more often and add organic matter for thirstier plants. Slower than 4 hours signals poor drainage — consider raised beds, added gypsum and compost, or choosing plants that tolerate wet feet.
